Before we begin I should mention that I'm an idiot and don't really know what I'm talking about. I'm just speaking from what I've seen and how websites and portfolios have benefitted people I know.

I would like to help my friends and anyone who knows me create an online presence because I think it is one of the most valuable **FREE** things you can make and use.

# The BIG issue

Since I first got a computer I've spent most of my time fiddling with things, making half-baked concepts, and generally creating a large pile of unfinished biznezz (aka doo-doo feces).

Most of the things I create are digital, but I never really considered that it might help to display and discuss my stuff in a way that fits me. I've always tried to fit my words and work around the limitations and formats of whatever platform I was using.

Have you ever tried posting something on Twitter that isn't just one image or 200 characters?

And following that thought: have you ever had to edit or change what you were posting because you knew the limitations of the platform you were using?

Those two questions made me realize I've been using the internet wrong for the last 10 years.

## Your own website

I don't *need* a website. The amount of stuff I make probably doesn't justify one.

Before Wix, GoDaddy, and Squarespace you had to do it yourself. Surprisingly, a lot of people who didn't really know what they were doing still managed it and carved out their own space. Because people were winging it, those websites had personality and flair.

I'm not saying websites don't have personality anymore—it's just that the handmade ones feel personal and special.

So you end up with a bunch of sites made by people doing wildly different things: writing, research, collecting information about hobbies, interests, current events, personal perspectives, and professional work.

How are all these people with different goals and skill levels able to create coherent places to show off their stuff without running into major issues?

When I first considered making a website, I had a few worries. The path of least resistance is to just use social platforms that already exist. That way you don't have to deal with the hassle of building a site just to get feedback or eyeballs on your stuff.

So before starting, I had a few questions.

### Some questions I wanted answered

**First of all:** how can someone who writes about the history of calculators use the same building blocks, tools, and techniques as someone making a site about their favorite types of ice to chew?

*Because websites are just documents. Pages, text, images. That's it.*

The tricky part is figuring out what you want to do and how you want to do it.

---

**Second of all:** what if my website looks bad?

I'm using this place to show the world what I do—what if my tiny nugget brain can't make something nice and approachable?

The best part about having a website is that you choose how it looks and flows. If you think it should look a certain way, then that's how it should look.

When I made this site I spent way too much time obsessing over pixel-perfect design. It should have been usable in 2–3 months, but I wasted most of that time polishing things that didn't matter.

Don't do that.

Focus on what the site needs to **do**, then worry about how it looks. Just like writing an essay: get the ideas out first, then format them. And if things go horribly wrong, you can always use a template.

Looks don't matter.

---

**Third of all:** if someone makes a website that fits their needs… do people actually visit it?

This thought stopped me from making a site for years. Why put all this effort into a website when you could get more feedback just using existing platforms?

But the reality is there's more to a personal site than the number of *hits* you get.

If people visit and read your stuff—great. You've created a resource others find value in.

But if nobody visits… what's the point?

Why not just put everything on social platforms?

Well here's my list of reasons why that question shouldn't even matter.

Just stop being a massive loser and make a website. (sorry)

### Personal archive

The biggest reason to create and maintain a website is **archival**.

Information disappears from search engines all the time. Platforms shut down. Accounts get banned or hacked.

A social platform could explode overnight and take all your memories, opinions, and work with it.

Your own website only explodes if **you** decide to blow it up.

Your site can act as a vault of information and data that is handcrafted and personal to you—your own one-stop shop.

### Self-reliance

You've probably heard about self-reliance in the context of collecting rainwater, generating energy, or growing food.

But having your own website is a form of self-reliance too.

Creating and maintaining a space that shows who you are, what you do, what you make, and what you think **without relying on external platforms** is self-reliance.

Your post on Facebook can be taken down.

Your post on your own website cannot.

### Created with you in mind

The goals of your website determine how it should work.

A photographer needs to display images nicely. Someone archiving old DOS games might care more about catalogs and descriptions.

The form and function of the site should serve **your needs**.

You don't have to reshape your thinking or work to fit a platform anymore. The best version of your work can live on your site, and you can share links or previews elsewhere.

### Milestones and progress

This one is big for me.

Looking back at something you made a year ago is a great way to see how far you've come.

Just like a diary shows what you were thinking at the time, a website lets you document and showcase what you've made over time.

Who doesn't like seeing a little bit of progress?

### Accountability

I have a personal rule for posting online that I'll explain later: I don't delete posts, comments, replies, or opinions.

You don't have to follow that rule, but it forces a certain level of responsibility.

When something you post is visible to strangers—not just friends—you naturally put more effort into what you're saying or showing.

That pressure alone has helped me actually finish projects.

### Freedom of speech and expression

Owning your website means you can say and do what you want with it.

You can share ideas, opinions, and perspectives without worrying about moderation policies or platform rules changing overnight.

When a small group of users can get your account removed, it's worth asking whether that platform is the best place to store your work.

### Middle out

Up until now it might sound like I'm anti-social-media. I'm not. It's a useful tool.

I just think people rely on it too much.

If your account gets locked, hacked, banned, or the platform dies, all your work disappears.

The **middle-out** approach fixes that.

The idea is simple:

- Create and host things on your website.
- You always own the original.
- Share a link, preview, or copy on other platforms.
- Always link back to the original.
- Those sites spread the content, but your site is the source.

Simple, but now you've removed your dependence on those platforms while still using them.

## Goals for my website

You've either read everything up to this point like a freak, or you've skimmed straight here and are reading this sentence right now. Both options weird me out.

I've never really written *to* people before, so hopefully I get better at it.

Before talking about my goals, I'll say it again for anyone who hasn't absorbed it yet:

**Just make a website.**

Use Wix if you want. I don't care. I'll probably make tutorials showing how to create and host one for free with minimal brain power.

A website is a dynamic thing. I'll be changing styles, adding sections, updating old posts, and implementing new features as I learn things.

But for the sake of future Ben, here's what I want this site to do:

- Show off the stupid stuff I'm doing.
- Recommend tools, software, resources, and workflows that have helped me.
- Talk about cool things I've found.
- Make tutorials on how to do certain thangs.
- Build up a collection of information and resources.

These are pretty generic for now, but hopefully I'll flesh them out later.

## Closing thinks

Having a personal site might be one of the most valuable things you can create right now.

Even if your hobbies aren't digital, being able to store a lifetime of resources, ideas, and knowledge in one place you control is incredibly useful.

You could literally fit your entire site on a 16GB USB stick and know exactly where everything is because **you built it**.

That's a lot easier than trying to find the scrap of paper you wrote something on four years ago.
